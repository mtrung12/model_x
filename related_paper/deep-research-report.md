# Textual Signals of Personality Traits: A Comprehensive Review

Personality manifests itself in language use: the words, style, and structure people use when communicating often reveal enduring traits. Decades of psycholinguistic and computational studies confirm that features ranging from choice of function words to narrative style can be indicative of the Big Five personality dimensions (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism)【48†L120-L124】【39†L411-L419】. Below we review major categories of textual cues – **surface features, lexical and syntactic style, sentiment and emotion, discourse and narrative patterns, topic content, argumentation style, and social/interpersonal language** – and explain how each correlates with personality. For each category we note (1) *why* and *which* traits it tends to reflect, (2) illustrative text examples, and (3) how it has been studied in psycholinguistics, computational linguistics, and NLP-based personality research. We also distinguish **surface features** (e.g. punctuation, emoji) from deeper **linguistic features** (syntax, semantics) and **cognitive-behavioral markers** (immediacy, argumentation patterns). Finally, we summarize the mapping of features to traits in a taxonomy (tables) and discuss limitations and ethical issues of inferring personality from text.

## Linguistic Style and Syntax

**Features:** sentence and clause complexity (mean sentence length, subordinate clauses), **lexical diversity** (unique word count, type-token ratio), complexity measures (big/small words, reading difficulty), **expressivity** (adjective/adverb usage)【5†L13-L21】【39†L457-L466】, and overall style consistency. For example, **lexical diversity** (using many unique, low-frequency words) often correlates with higher Openness and Conscientiousness, and lower Extraversion and Neuroticism【4†L25-L33】. In formal settings, extraverts tend to use simpler, more common words (lower diversity) than introverts【39†L443-L451】. By contrast, highly conscientious and open people often produce more complex syntax and varied vocabulary, reflecting careful detail and imagination【39†L457-L466】【45†L237-L245】. Mairesse *et al.* (2007) and Spitzley *et al.* (2022) found, for example, that _openness_ was associated with use of “big” words (6+ letters) and long sentences【23†L1071-L1078】【39†L457-L466】, while _conscientiousness_ was hypothesized to align with complexity and structure (though empirical links vary)【39†L457-L466】【45†L237-L245】. More expressive style (many adjectives/adverbs) has been tied to Extraversion (enthusiasm) and Openness (detail) in some contexts【5†L19-L28】, though findings can depend on speaking vs writing context.  

*Example:* A highly open/conscientious author might write “*We investigated numerous complex alternatives before settling on this strategy*,” whereas a more extraverted/intuitive speaker might favor shorter, simpler statements: “*This was a really exciting project; I loved working on it!*”.  

Psycholinguistic studies typically measure these features via transcripts or writing samples (e.g. Mairesse *et al.*’s studies of essays and dialogues【19†L7-L16】【23†L1071-L1078】). In computational linguistics, automatic tools (e.g. counting unique tokens, parsing syntax trees) quantify diversity and complexity across large corpora【39†L457-L466】【45†L218-L227】. In NLP/personality computing, lexical diversity and complexity often serve as features in machine-learning models; for instance, open-vocabulary models find topics and long-word usage predictive of Openness【23†L1071-L1078】【45†L218-L227】.  

## Word Choice and Vocabulary Richness

**Features:** choice of specific content words and word-level statistics (vocabulary sophistication). This includes the use of rare or specialized terms, jargon, or indicator words (from lexica like LIWC), as well as measures like Type-Token Ratio or the use of big/unique words. Individuals high in **Openness** tend to use a wide and sophisticated vocabulary (many unique and long words), reflecting imagination and broad interests【23†L1071-L1078】【39†L457-L466】. Conversely, Extraverts often use shorter, high-frequency words (e.g. “good”, “nice”) and less lexical variety【39†L443-L451】【45†L237-L245】. Neurotic individuals may show reduced lexical richness (perhaps due to cognitive load of anxiety)【39†L443-L451】. Rich emotion vocabularies (especially for nuanced feeling words) correspond to *experience-seeking* and high Openness【10†L1-L4】【48†L120-L124】. Additionally, the use of social-category words (e.g. *family, friend, coworker*) signals interpersonal focus often seen in **Agreeableness** and **Extraversion**【39†L411-L419】. In sum, vocabulary reflects trait-level focus: conscientious planners may use achievement- and task-related words; open individuals mention abstract or aesthetic terms; extraverts name people and events; neurotics focus inward on self-related terms.  

*Example:* A writer scoring high on Openness might say “*I’m fascinated by the concept of existentialism and read philosophical tomes.*” (uncommon terms “existentialism”, “tomes”), whereas a high-Neuroticism author might write “*I’m worried all the time, anxious about everything.*” (simpler words but heavy negative emotional content).  

Psycholinguistically, vocabulary patterns have been examined via diaries and free essays (Pennebaker & King 1999; Mairesse *et al.* 2007)【48†L120-L124】【20†L938-L943】. Computationally, lexical features are extracted via dictionaries (LIWC categories, sentiment lexicons) or via open-vocabulary mining (topic models, word embeddings)【45†L218-L227】【47†L1-L4】. Modern NLP studies compare closed-vocabulary (handcrafted lists) vs open-vocabulary methods: large-scale analyses (e.g. Schwartz *et al.* 2013) show open-vocab topics and specialized terms markedly improve trait prediction (e.g. topic models picked up hobbies indicating Openness)【45†L237-L245】【48†L120-L124】.  

## Sentiment and Emotional Tone

**Features:** proportion and intensity of positive vs negative emotion words, and overall sentiment polarity. Emotional tone is a strong cue: **Extraversion** correlates with abundant positive-affect words (e.g. *great, happy, excited*), whereas **Neuroticism** correlates with negative-affect and anxiety terms (e.g. *sad, anxious, hate*)【48†L120-L124】【39†L411-L419】. Agreeable people also tend to use more positive-emotion words and fewer anger or swear words【39†L411-L419】【22†L1001-L1010】. Conscientiousness shows a distinct lack of negative language: high-Conscientiousness authors use fewer curse words and less negativity【39†L411-L419】. In practice, sentiment analysis tools applied to text have found that people who use many emotionally charged words are often high on extraversion or neuroticism (depending on polarity)【48†L120-L124】【31†L13-L16】. For example, one study of messaging found extraverts prefer positive emojis (😃) while neurotics favor “intense” emotional emojis (😰,😭)【31†L13-L16】.  

*Example:* “*I’m absolutely thrilled and ecstatic about our success!*” (high positive tone, likely Extraversion) vs “*I’m really worried... This is making me so anxious and frustrated.*” (high negative tone, likely Neuroticism). 

Psychological studies (e.g. Pennebaker and King 1999) first noted these links in essays【48†L120-L124】; more recent SNS analyses reaffirm them (Schwartz *et al.*; Eichstaedt *et al.*). Computational linguists typically score text on affective lexica or use sentiment classifiers to quantify emotional tone, then correlate scores with self-reported traits【48†L120-L124】【31†L13-L16】. In NLP/personality computing, sentiment counts are standard features for models: e.g. Higher count of positive-emotion LIWC words predicts higher Extraversion; higher negative-emotion counts predict higher Neuroticism【48†L120-L124】【39†L411-L419】. 

## Discourse and Narrative Patterns

**Features:** higher-level organization of text – narrative structure, coherence, **verbal immediacy** (use of personal, concrete language), pacing, and discourse markers. For example, **verbal immediacy** is defined as frequent first-person pronouns, present-tense verbs, and few articles/long words【26†L7-L16】. Highly immediate style (“I did this,” active concrete present) tends to signal a personal, involved stance. In *public* contexts (conversation or social media), high immediacy signals **Extraversion** (enthusiastic, engaged talker); in *private* contexts (journaling), it signals **Neuroticism** (anxious inner focus)【26†L1-L4】【26†L85-L89】. Indeed, Mehl *et al.* (2006) found that immediate, personal language was indicative of extraversion in everyday speech but of neuroticism in private writings【26†L1-L4】【26†L85-L89】. Narrative style also varies: extraverts often tell vivid, action-oriented stories about social events; high-Openness individuals construct imaginative, metaphorical narratives; agreeable people emphasize collective actions (“we did this”) and positive outcomes; whereas low-Aggreebility individuals’ discourse may be more confrontational. 

*Example:* A highly extraverted, agreeable person might describe a party: “*Yesterday I went to this great concert with my friends – it was amazing, and we had such a fun time singing and dancing!*”. A low-agreeable (disagreeable) narrator might say: “*I had dinner with them, but frankly they were boring and I made sure to tell everyone my point of view.*” 

Discourse features are studied in psycholinguistics via conversation transcripts and narrative tasks (e.g. analyzing stream-of-consciousness writing)【26†L49-L58】. Psychologists also examine how presence of hedges (“maybe”), intensifiers, or narrative cohesion (repetition, connectors like because/however) relate to traits. In computational research, discourse signals are gleaned through “immediacy scores,” speech-act tagging, or by tracking pronoun shifts over discourse【26†L7-L16】【25†L376-L384】. NLP systems may include features like frequency of causal words (“because”), narrative verbs (“remember, realize”), or topic continuity. Research finds, for instance, that more insightful/causal words signal Openness and Conscientiousness, reflecting analytical narrative【23†L1075-L1078】, while repetitive topic shifts or filler phrases may correlate with anxiety. 

## Pronouns, Tense, and Modality

**Features:** use of personal pronouns (I, you, we, they), verb tense (past/present/future), and modal verbs (should, might, could). Pronoun usage powerfully reflects focus and social orientation. In general, **high Neuroticism** correlates with lots of first-person singular pronouns (“I, me, my”) – an inward focus and anxiety【48†L120-L124】【39†L411-L419】. Indeed, individuals high in anxiety or depression use “I” far more frequently【48†L120-L124】. **Extraverts** tend to use more first-person plural (“we, our”) and second-person (“you”) pronouns, reflecting a social and inclusive perspective【39†L411-L419】【23†L1071-L1078】. For example, Mairesse *et al.* found extraverts often say “we” to involve others, and used more social words overall【23†L1071-L1078】. **Openness** often shows low pronoun usage: open, abstract thinkers use more articles/prepositions (focusing on descriptions) and fewer self-references【39†L411-L419】【23†L1071-L1078】. Modality and tense add nuance: frequent future-tense or modal verbs (planning language: “will”, “should”) can signal Conscientiousness and goal-orientation, whereas overuse of “would/might” (tentative language) may correlate with Neuroticism or low Conscientiousness (uncertainty). Present-tense narration tends to feel more immediate and is often used by extraverts (storytelling in real time)【26†L7-L16】.  

*Example:* “*I think we should try this strategy. We will succeed if we all work together.*” (inclusive “we”, future tense, tentative “should” – suggests Extraversion/Agreeableness and planning) versus “*I don’t feel comfortable doing that. Maybe it’s not a good idea.*” (repeated “I”, modal “maybe” – suggests Neuroticism, low Agreeableness). 

Psychologists have long noted pronoun–personality links in controlled essays and diaries【48†L120-L124】【39†L411-L419】. Computational work extracts pronoun ratios as features (e.g. LIWC “Pronoun”, “I”, “We” categories) and finds significant correlations: higher “I” predicts Neuroticism【48†L120-L124】, higher “we/you” predicts Extraversion/Agreeableness【39†L411-L419】【23†L1071-L1078】. Tense and modal markers are studied via tagging: e.g., Pennebaker’s *immediacy* factors combine present tense with first-person pronouns and few articles【26†L7-L16】. Modern NLP pipelines similarly include POS tags and dependency patterns to capture modality and tense as numeric features for machine learning. 

## Punctuation, Emojis, and Formatting

**Features:** punctuation usage (exclamation “!”, question “?”, ellipses “…”, capitalization), emoticons/emojis, and text formatting quirks (all-caps, elongated letters). These paralinguistic markers substitute for nonverbal cues and reflect personality. For instance, frequent exclamation points and emojis often indicate high positive affect and sociability: extraverts and high-Agreeableness individuals tend to use **more emoticons/emojis** (especially positive-face emojis) and exclamation marks to convey enthusiasm【29†L1-L4】【31†L13-L16】. Indeed, Hall and Pennington (2013) found emoticon frequency positively associated with Extraversion【29†L1-L4】. Conversely, **Neuroticism** has been linked to more strategic emoji use: anxious users may use emojis/stickers to manage social discomfort (e.g. to soften statements)【31†L37-L42】【31†L65-L73】. One study showed neurotics more often used emojis to “avoid awkwardness” in conversation, whereas extraverts did so less【31†L37-L42】. High Conscientiousness correlates with **fewer** casual symbols (less LOL or emoji, more standard grammar), while low Conscientiousness correlates with more “sloppy” punctuation (multiple exclaims, ellipses) and emotive emoji usage. All-caps or multiple exclamation marks (“!!!!”) often signal strong arousal: some link intense punctuation to Irritability or even low Agreeableness (if used in anger). 

*Example:* An extraverted friendly texter might write: “*Had an AMAZING day at the beach!!! 😀🌊 Can’t wait to go back!!!*” (many exclamation points, positive emoji). A more anxious user might write: “*I… um… I’m not sure about that idea… 😬*” (hesitations, ellipses, uneasy emoji). 

Psycholinguistic research on “textisms” shows that punctuation and emoji carry social meaning in CMC (e.g. ending a text with a period can seem abrupt)【32†L53-L62】. Personality studies have empirically linked emoji/punctuation to traits【29†L1-L4】【31†L13-L16】. NLP approaches automatically count emojis and punctuation features; for example, one large survey found overall emoji use correlated positively with Agreeableness and Neuroticism but negatively with Conscientiousness【31†L13-L16】. Emoji-processing models even identify clusters of emoji whose usage correlates with Extraversion, Neuroticism, or Agreeableness【29†L1-L4】. In practice, modern text-based personality detectors include features like “# of emoji”, distribution of punctuation types, and known emoticon lexicons. These surface features, though shallow, encode emotional emphasis and social style that strongly modulate perceived personality.

## Topics and Semantic Fields

**Features:** subject matter and thematic vocabulary. What people write *about* often reflects interests and values linked to personality. For example, high **Openness** individuals frequently mention art, science, travel, and abstract ideas; high **Extraversion** people discuss social events, parties, and friendships; high **Conscientiousness** writers mention work, planning, tasks, and health; high **Agreeableness** people emphasize family, relationships, harmony; and high **Neuroticism** writers focus on self, health anxieties, negative life events. In LIWC terms, extraverts score high on “Social” and “Friends” categories; agreeable individuals use more “Family” and “Affiliation” terms【39†L411-L419】; conscientious individuals use more “Achievement” or “Work” words; and neurotics use more “Affective” (sad, anxiety) and self-referential categories【48†L120-L124】【39†L411-L419】. Semantic coherence over paragraphs (e.g. staying on topic) may also vary: agreeable/conscientious writers may stick to the point, while neurotic/disagreeable may ramble or switch topics under stress.

*Example:* A blog post by an open-minded reader might say: “*I spent the weekend exploring a modern art exhibit and attending a science lecture.*” (creative/artistic topics). An extravert’s post: “*Went clubbing with friends, danced all night!*”. A conscientious journal: “*I organized my schedule, created a to-do list for the week, and read a time-management book.*” 

Psychological research has long observed thematic differences. Hirsh & Peterson (2009) and Ireland & Mehl (2014) summarize that agreeable people use more words about social and home life and fewer money or death terms, while open individuals use more abstract and referential words (articles, prepositions)【39†L411-L419】. Corpus linguists and NLP techniques (topic modeling, LDA) have been used in personality computing to find topical clusters predictive of traits. For instance, Schwartz *et al.* (2013) used open-vocabulary mining on Facebook and discovered that frequent discussion of travel and culture predicted Openness, whereas frequent self-disclosure predicted Neuroticism【45†L218-L227】【48†L120-L124】. Semantic embeddings can capture subtle cues (e.g. mention of “forest” and “imagination” vs “restaurant” and “friends”). In practice, content features (bag-of-words, topics) are standard in computational personality models, reflecting that people’s interests and recurring themes in their writing echo their personality profile.

## Argumentation and Reasoning Patterns

**Features:** markers of logical structure, evidence use, and persuasive language. This includes words like “because,” “thus,” “therefore,” and explicit argumentative moves. Individuals high in **Openness** and **Conscientiousness** often display more structured reasoning: they may use more causal/connective words (since, because), qualifiers (“clearly,” “certainly”), and tend to build logical arguments. In contrast, high **Extraversion** and **Agreeableness** may frame arguments in personal or social terms (“I think”, “we all know”) rather than purely logical stance. Low Agreeableness (hostility) might use more confrontational or negation (“but actually,” “no,” “wrong”) as they challenge others. **Neurotic** individuals might hedge more (“maybe,” “I guess”), reflecting uncertainty. Research on “cognitive complexity” finds that higher openness is linked to more causation and insight words (analytical style)【23†L1075-L1078】, whereas simple or affect-driven rhetoric suggests neurotic or low-conscientious style. Rhetorical questions (e.g. “How could we ignore that?”) and metaphors also reflect creativity/Openness.  

*Example:* A conscientious/openness profile might write: “*Given that sales have declined over the last two quarters, it is logical to conclude that adjusting our marketing strategy will improve outcomes.*” (explicit logic). A more neurotic speaker: “*Um, I’m not sure why this happened, but maybe we should think about doing something different?*” (hesitant, tentative).

These features are less studied than lexicon but appear in discourse analyses. Psycholinguists examine complexity of reasoning (fact vs feeling, analytic vs narrative) and relate them to traits. Some personality computing studies include LIWC “cause”, “insight”, “certainty” categories; for example, use of insight words has been associated with Openness and Conscientiousness in group dialogues【23†L1075-L1078】. NLP frameworks for discourse (e.g. argument mining, rhetorical structure theory) are beginning to be applied: a model might detect the prevalence of justification phrases (“because”) or the use of argument markers, then correlate these with personality labels. While this area is emerging, the consensus is that more argumentative, analytic language implies a systematic, conscientious/intellectual mindset, whereas disorganized or purely emotional argumentation suggests affect-driven (neurotic or impulsive) thinking.  

## Social and Interpersonal Language

**Features:** language that signals one’s social orientation – how one relates to others through language. This includes use of polite forms, inclusive pronouns, social greetings (“hi everyone”), hedging vs directness, and alignment words (like mirroring the other’s terms). Highly **Agreeable** and **Extraverted** individuals tend to use more communal, affiliative language: they say “we” and “our” to emphasize group identity【39†L411-L419】【23†L1071-L1078】, compliment or encourage others, and use polite/disclaiming phrases (“please,” “maybe,” “sorry” when criticizing). They reference social groups (“our team”, “friends”) and positive social emotions (“excited to see you”). By contrast, low-Agreeable (antagonistic) people use more “I” to assert themselves and may drop politeness formulas. High Agreeableness also correlates with **fewer** negative/social-disharmony terms (cursing, hostility)【39†L411-L419】. In gender-norm studies of language, it’s noted that friends may use more exclamation and self-disclosure; similarly, extraverts engage readers by asking questions or using interactive address (“you”). Modesty markers (“just”, “maybe”) might indicate agreeableness and humility, whereas boastful tone (“certainly, of course”) could signal dominance (lower agreeableness). 

*Example:* An agreeable social post: “*Hey team, thanks so much for all your help on this project. I really appreciate it and I’m excited about our results!*” (includes “I” and “our,” positive, thankful tone). A less agreeable, more distant post: “*I completed the report. The results are listed below.*” (lack of collegial language, terse).

Studies of social orientation in text often focus on pronoun use and social word counts. As noted, LIWC’s “social processes” category (friend, family, speak) tends to be higher for agreeable/extraverted people【39†L411-L419】. Psycholinguistics examines narratives for social focus (e.g. how often people mention interactions vs objects). Computationally, features like direct address (count of “you”), greetings/farewells, and discourse markers (“um”, “okay?”, laughter tokens) can be mined. For example, use of “we” and communal references is an important feature in personality classifiers【23†L1071-L1078】【39†L411-L419】. Social network analyses also combine text with interaction patterns: someone whose language emphasizes connection often scores high on Agreeableness/Extraversion.

## Limitations and Ethical Considerations

Inferring personality from text has significant **limitations**. The observed correlations are typically modest (often r≈0.2–0.4)【45†L237-L245】 and context-dependent. For instance, the same individual may use more negative words in private diaries than on public social media【39†L419-L427】. Language reflects not only stable traits but also mood, social role, and situation. Models often generalize poorly across contexts or cultures. Many studies rely on self-reported personality scores, which themselves have biases. Importantly, most work focuses on aggregate tendencies in large groups; applying these to a single individual can be very inaccurate. As Park *et al.* note, the psychometric validity and reliability of language-based personality assessments (LBAs) must be established – e.g. whether LBAs truly discriminate traits as intended and remain stable over time【45†L253-L261】.  

**Ethical concerns** are paramount. Textual personality analysis can infringe privacy: individuals often do not consent to profiling, yet their language betrays personal attributes. Misuse could occur in employment (screening candidates by writing), marketing (targeting ads), or surveillance (inference without user knowledge). There is a risk of **labeling and self-fulfilling biases**: once a trait is predicted, others might treat the person accordingly. Models may also be biased: they often reflect the cultures and demographics of the training data (e.g. mostly Western social media users), so they may mis-interpret language from different groups. Moreover, inferring traits that individuals consider “private” (e.g. mental health, political orientation) raises ethical flags. Research ethics guidelines (and regulations like the GDPR) demand transparency, consent, and careful handling of such inferences. 

Finally, personality itself is complex and malleable. A text snapshot captures one of many facets of a person. As both psychological theory and practice recognize, any text-based personality score should be used with caution, as one of multiple signals, not a definitive verdict. Any deployment of such technology must balance innovation with respect for individual autonomy, avoid over-reliance on imperfect predictions, and ensure data security and fairness.
